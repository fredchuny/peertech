Mastering Spreadsheets: Your Easy & Fun Guide!

Learn Excel and other spreadsheets with fun examples and simple tips.

Welcome to Spreadsheet Fun!

Welcome! This website helps you learn Excel and other spreadsheet programs in a fun, hands-on way. Whether you're just starting or want to get better, our easy examples and clear steps will help you. We believe the best way to learn is by doing, so let's jump in!

Spreadsheets are super helpful tools for putting data in order, doing math, and looking at information. From keeping track of your daily spending to managing big projects, knowing how to use spreadsheets is a great skill to have. Let's begin!

Fun Practice Activities!

Practice is key! Try these examples in your own spreadsheet program (like Microsoft Excel, Google Sheets, or LibreOffice Calc).

Easy Examples: Getting Started

Easy

1. Royal Castle Inventory: Basic Typing & Making Things Look Good

You're in charge of all the treasures and supplies in a grand castle! Let's put your royal items and feast ingredients in order using basic spreadsheet skills.

Goal: Make a simple list of castle items and make them look nice.

  1. Open a new spreadsheet: Start a fresh, empty sheet.
  2. Set up Column Titles:
    • In cell A1, type "Castle Item".
    • In cell B1, type "How Many (Units)".
    • In cell C1, type "Last Time Stocked".
  3. Basic Typing:
    • In cell A2, type "Dragon Scales".
    • In cell B2, type "500".
    • In cell C2, type "2025-07-28".
    • Add a few more items below "Dragon Scales" (like "Magic Wands", "Sparkling Potions") with how many you have and their dates.
  4. Making Things Look Good (Formatting):
    • Pick cells A1 to C1 (these are your titles).
    • Make them Bold.
    • Pick cell A2 ("Dragon Scales"). Make it Italic.
    • Highlight cell B2 ("500") with a light background color (like light yellow, like gold!).
  5. Date Formatting:
    • Pick the cells in your "Last Time Stocked" column (like C2:C5).
    • Right-click on the cells you picked and choose "Format Cells..." (or "Format" > "Number" > "Date" in Google Sheets).
    • Change the date to something fun like "July 28, 2025" or "28-Jul-2025".
  6. Understanding Cells:
    • Find cell C5. What's written in it?
    • If your castle had a super big list, where would cell AA3 be? (Hint: after column Z, it starts with AA, AB, and so on.)
    • Why are cell names important? (They help formulas find the right numbers, like knowing exactly where your "Sparkling Potions" count is!)
  7. Changing Column/Row Size:
    • Move your mouse between column titles A and B. Your mouse will change to a double arrow. Click and drag to make column A wider.
    • Or, right-click on a column title (like A) and pick "Column Width..." to type in a number for its size.
    • Do the same for row height (between row numbers 1 and 2).
    • Auto-adjust: Double-click the line between two column titles (like A and B) to make the column just wide enough for what's inside.
  8. Add a New Row for a Title (Just before Merging!):
    • Right-click on the row number 1 (on the far left).
    • Choose "Insert" or "Insert Row Above". This will make a new, empty row at the very top. Your old titles will now be in Row 2.
  9. Merge Cells (Combine Cells for a Big Title):
    • Pick cells A1 to C1 (the new, empty row you just added).
    • Find the "Merge & Center" button (it's usually in the "Home" tab, under "Alignment"). Click it. This joins the cells.
    • Now, type "Grand Castle Inventory Report" in the newly merged cell A1. This makes a nice big title for your whole list!
  10. Table Styling (Making a Table Look Good):
    • Pick all your data (from your new title, like A1:C6, or just your data and headers, like A2:C6).
    • Go to "Format as Table" (in Excel) or "Format" > "Alternating colors" (in Google Sheets) to pick ready-made table styles. Make your castle inventory look amazing!
  11. Challenge: Now, try to make this royal inventory all by yourself from the start!

2. Bakery Treat Tracker: Making Your List & AutoFill Fun!

You run a super popular bakery! Help keep track of how many delicious treats you bake each day. It's about making a neat, clear list, and letting Excel do some work for you!

Goal: Make a simple list for bakery treat costs and use AutoFill.

  1. Set up Columns (Make your main titles):
    • In cell A1, type "Day of the Week".
    • In cell B1, type "Cost of Ingredients ($)".
  2. Enter Data (Put in your information):
    • In cell A2, type "Monday".
    • In cell A3, type "Tuesday".
    • In cell A4, type "Wednesday".
    • In cell B2, type "50".
    • In cell B3, type "65".
    • In cell B4, type "70".
  3. Autofill Days (Fill in days automatically):
    • Pick cells A2 to A4 ("Monday" to "Wednesday").
    • Drag the small square (fill handle) at the bottom-right corner of cell A4 down to A6. This should automatically fill in "Thursday" and "Friday".
    • Put in ingredient costs for Thursday (like 55) and Friday (like 80) in cells B5 and B6 respectively.
  4. Autofill Numbers (Fill in numbers automatically):
    • In cell A10, type "1".
    • In cell A11, type "2".
    • Pick both cells A10 and A11. Drag the small square (fill handle) at the bottom-right corner downwards. What happens? (It should fill in 3, 4, 5, etc. – perfect for numbering your baking batches!)
  5. Make Titles Stand Out:
    • Pick cells A1 and B1.
    • Make them Bold.
    • Use the "Center" button to put the text in the middle of these cells.
  6. Great job! You just made your first simple list for bakery treats and used AutoFill. This organized info is what makes spreadsheets so useful, even for delicious pastries!

6. Bakery Treat Tracker: Formulas in Action!

Let's keep working on our bakery treat tracker! Now we'll add smart formulas to figure out the total cost of ingredients and the average daily cost.

Goal: Add up and find the average of ingredient costs, and total cost for each day.

Remember: The equal sign (=) is the magic button that tells Excel you want it to do math!

  1. Start with your "Bakery Treat Tracker" from Example 2. Make sure you have data for Monday to Friday in columns A and B.
  2. Calculate Total Ingredient Cost:
    • In cell A7, type "Total Ingredient Cost".
    • In cell B7, type =SUM(
    • Then, drag your mouse from cell B2 down to B6 (this picks the group of cells B2:B6).
    • Type the closing curvy bracket ) and press Enter.
    • You just made your first formula! The total cost of ingredients for the week should show up.
  3. See the Formula Magic:
    • Change the ingredient cost for Wednesday in cell B4 from "70" to "90".
    • Notice how the "Total Ingredient Cost" in cell B7 changes by itself! This is why formulas are so powerful – no more counting by hand for your delicious treats!
  4. Calculate Average Daily Cost:
    • In cell A8, type "Average Daily Cost".
    • In cell B8, type =AVERAGE(B2:B6) and press Enter. This will show the average amount spent on ingredients each day.
  5. Add "Number of Treats Baked" Column:
    • In cell C1, type "Treats Baked".
    • In cells C2 to C5, type "100".
    • In cell C6, type "120".
  6. Calculate "Total Cost per Day" Column:
    • In cell D1, type "Total Cost per Day".
    • In cell D2, type =B2*C2 and press Enter. (This multiplies the ingredient cost by how many treats were baked for Monday).
    • Pick cell D2. Drag the small square (fill handle) at the bottom-right corner of cell D2 down to D6. This will automatically fill the formula for all the other days.
  7. Calculate Grand Total for "Total Cost per Day":
    • In cell D7, type =SUM(D2:D6) and press Enter.

7. Detective Agency Case Stats

You're the smart leader of a detective agency! Look at your team's case results to find out the total mysteries solved, average clues found, and bonus points earned.

Goal: Use SUM, AVERAGE, and multiply to check detective cases.

  1. Set up Data:
    • In A1, type "Detective Name".
    • In B1, type "Mysteries Solved".
    • In A2, type "Detective Smith". In B2, type "15".
    • In A3, type "Agent Jones". In B3, type "22".
    • In A4, type "Sleuth Miller". In B4, type "18".
    • In A5, type "Inspector Davis". In B5, type "25".
  2. Calculate Total Mysteries Solved:
    • In A7, type "Total Mysteries".
    • In B7, type =SUM(B2:B5).
  3. Calculate Average Mysteries Solved:
    • In A8, type "Average Mysteries per Detective".

  4. Calculate Bonus Points: (Let's say you get 10 bonus points for every 5 mysteries solved)
    • In C1, type "Bonus Points (per 5 mysteries)".
    • In C2, type =INT(B2/5)*10. (INT means it rounds down to a whole number).

  5. Bold Headers: Make your titles bold so the Chief can see them clearly!

You run a fun toy store, and you want to quickly see which toys are selling super well and which are... gathering dust. Use special coloring to automatically highlight your best-selling toys!

Goal: Make toy sales different colors based on how well they sell.

  1. Create Toy Sales Data:
    • In A1, type "Toy Name".
    • In B1, type "Units Sold This Month".
    • In A2, type "Robot Buddy". In B2, type "150".
    • In A3, type "Magic Slime". In B3, type "220".
    • In A4, type "Building Blocks". In B4, type "80".
    • In A5, type "Stuffed Animals". In B5, type "250".
  2. Apply Special Coloring (Conditional Formatting):
    • Pick the "Units Sold This Month" column (like B2:B5).

  3. See the Colors: Cells with units sold above 200 should be green, and those below 100 should be red. This quickly shows you your best and slowest-selling toys!

10. Recipe Finder: Finding Info with VLOOKUP/XLOOKUP

You're a master chef, and you need to quickly find ingredients for your amazing recipes from a big cookbook spreadsheet. Learn how to find specific info from a big list!

Goal: Learn how to pull information (ingredients) from another list.

Idea: Imagine you have a list of recipes with their names and main ingredients. You want to quickly find the main ingredient just by knowing the recipe name. That's what VLOOKUP (or the newer XLOOKUP) does!

  1. Make a "Recipe Book" List (Sheet2):
    • Open a new sheet (like Sheet2).
    • In A1, type "Recipe Name". In B1, type "Main Ingredient".
    • Put in some recipes:
      • A2: "Chocolate Cake", B2: "Cocoa Powder"
      • A3: "Spicy Chili", B3: "Ground Beef"
      • A4: "Lemonade", B4: "Lemons"
  2. Make a "Today's Menu" List (Sheet1):
    • Go back to your first sheet (like Sheet1).
    • In A10, type "Recipe for Today". In B10, type "Main Ingredient Needed".
    • In A11, type "Spicy Chili".
  3. Use VLOOKUP (for older Excel) or XLOOKUP (for newer Excel/Google Sheets):
    • In cell B11, type this formula: 
      =VLOOKUP(A11,Sheet2!A:B,2,FALSE)

      What it means:
      A11: What you're looking for (the Recipe Name).
      Sheet2!A:B: Where to look (all of columns A and B on Sheet2 - your Recipe Book).
      2: Which column to get the answer from in your cookbook (the 2nd column, which is Main Ingredient).
      FALSE: Means find an exact match.

      If you have XLOOKUP (it's better for newer versions):

      =XLOOKUP(A11,Sheet2!A:A,Sheet2!B:B)

      What it means:
      A11: What you're looking for (the Recipe Name).
      Sheet2!A:A: Where to look for the Recipe Name (column A on Sheet2).
      Sheet2!B:B: The column to get the answer from (column B on Sheet2).

  4. Change Recipe: Change "Spicy Chili" in A11 to "Chocolate Cake" or "Lemonade". See how the main ingredient changes by itself!

11. Garden Plant Tracker: Making Sure Data is Right

As a skilled gardener, you need to make sure you plant the right types of seeds in your garden. Set up rules to control what people type in, so you don't accidentally plant a cactus where your tomatoes should be!

Goal: Make a list that people can pick from for plant types.

  1. Make a List of Good Plants:
    • In a different part of your sheet (like in column F), make a list of approved plants:
      • F1: "Tomato"
      • F2: "Carrot"
      • F3: "Lettuce"
      • F4: "Sunflower"
  2. Apply Data Validation (Set Rules for Data):
    • Pick the cells where you want to use these rules (like A2:A10 in a new "Plant Type" column).

      • Under "Allow:", choose "List".

  3. Test It: Now, when you click on any cell where you set the rules, you should see a small arrow. Click it to choose from your list of good plants! Try typing something that's not on the list (like "Moon Rock"); Excel should show you an error, keeping your garden perfect!

Excel Tips for Working Faster

Freeze Panes (Lock Rows/Columns)

When you have a very long list of data, scrolling down can make you lose sight of your column titles. Freeze Panes (in the "View" tab > "Freeze Panes") lets you keep certain rows or columns always visible while you scroll through the rest of your data. Super helpful for big tables!

Absolute References ($) (Locking Cells in Formulas)

When you copy formulas, sometimes you want a cell to stay the same. Use the dollar sign ($) before the column letter and/or row number (like $A$1). Press F4 after picking a cell in a formula to change how it's locked (or not locked).

Text to Columns (Splitting Text)

If you have words in one cell that you want to split into many columns (like "John Doe" into "John" and "Doe"), use Text to Columns (in the "Data" tab > "Text to Columns"). You can split by a separator (like a comma or a space) or by a set width.

Remove Duplicates (Get Rid of Copies)

Tired of having the same things listed twice? Pick your data and use Remove Duplicates (in the "Data" tab > "Remove Duplicates") to quickly clean up your list. You can choose which columns to check for copies.

Flash Fill (Excel 2013+) (Smart Filling)

This amazing tool (in the "Data" tab > "Flash Fill" or press Ctrl+E) smartly fills in data based on a pattern you start. For example, if you have a column of full names and you type only the first name in the next column for the first person, Flash Fill can automatically get all the first names for you!

Must-Know Excel Shortcuts

Learning a few quick ways to do things can make you much faster in Excel. Here are some very helpful ones:

  • Ctrl + C: Copy what you've picked.
  • Ctrl + V: Paste what you copied.
  • Ctrl + X: Cut what you've picked (moves it).
  • Ctrl + Z: Undo your last action.
  • Ctrl + Y: Do again what you just undid.
  • F2: Edit the cell you're in.
  • Ctrl + S: Save your work.
  • Ctrl + A: Pick everything on the sheet.
  • Ctrl + B: Make text bold or un-bold.
  • Ctrl + I: Make text italic or un-italic.
  • Ctrl + U: Make text underlined or un-underlined.
  • Ctrl + Shift + L: Turn filters on or off for a group of cells.
  • Ctrl + Page Up / Page Down: Switch between your different sheets.
  • Ctrl + Arrow Keys: Jump to the edge of your data.
  • Shift + Arrow Keys: Make your selection bigger by one cell.
  • Ctrl + Shift + Arrow Keys: Make your selection bigger all the way to the last cell with data.
